                        <p>In Brunswick, Georgia, a predominantly Black community lives in the shadow of industrial
                            facilities, where decades of conventional environmental advocacy have failed to create
                            meaningful change.</p>



                        <p>This isn&#8217;t just an anomaly. It&#8217;s a pattern repeated across the American South,
                            where traditional climate solutions often overlook the complexities of environmental impact,
                            public health, and community prosperity. From industrial pollution to basic infrastructure,
                            the environmental burden on Black communities in the South often manifests in health
                            disparities:</p>



                        <ul class="wp-block-list">
                            <li>The area from Baton Rouge to New Orleans has been dubbed Cancer Alley due to its
                                abundance of petrochemical plants and refineries.
                                <strong>The census tract within this area (St. John Parish) has the&nbsp;<a href="https://www.hrw.org/report/2024/01/25/were-dying-here/fight-life-louisiana-fossil-fuel-sacrifice-zone">highest cancer risk</a>&nbsp;from industrial air pollution in the United States &#8211; more than seven times the national average</strong>
                            </li>



                            <li>While Alabama&#8217;s Black Belt makes up less than 0.2% of the U.S. population,
                                <strong>it accounts for over&nbsp;<a href="https://southern-science.com/2023/09/09/state-to-begin-door-to-door-survey-on-lowndes-sewage-issues/">12%</a>&nbsp;of Americans lacking&nbsp;<a href="https://www.epa.gov/water-infrastructure/closing-americas-wastewater-access-gap">basic water and plumbing systems</a></strong>
                            </li>



                            <li>Nationally, Black Americans are more likely to die from stroke than white Americans
                                &#8211;
                                <strong>but that disparity is between <a href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1995237/#:~:text=Results,than%20in%20non%2Dsouthern%20states.">6% and 21%</a>&nbsp;higher in Southern states</strong>
                            </li>
                        </ul>



                        <p>However, these communities have gained a fighting partner in their corner. In Atlanta&#8217;s
                            Partnership for Southern Equity (PSE), a nonprofit organization that advances racial equity
                            and shared prosperity in the American South, an innovative new approach to environmental
                            justice is emerging.</p>



                        <p>Nathanial Smith, founder and Chief Equity Officer of PSE, puts it bluntly when asked why he
                            thinks Black communities are disproportionately impacted. &#8220;For people in particular
                            communities of color, racism is the social determinant of health,” says Smith. “Racism has
                            facilitated environments that continue to harm historically disinvested communities of
                            color.&#8221;</p>



                        <p>Smith’s organization aims to change the South’s attitude towards environmental issues
                            affecting marginalized communities and is already producing measurable results by connecting
                            environmental justice directly to community health and generational wealth.</p>



                        <p>This is how PSE is taking action.</p>



                        <h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-battle-for-brunswick">The Battle For Brunswick</h2>



                        <p>Brunswick has 14 sites on Georgia&#8217;s Hazardous Sites Inventory—including four Superfund
                            sites (areas designated by the government to contain chemicals toxic to humans), more than
                            any other Georgia city. The danger these sites pose was illustrated in November 2023, when a
                            chemical fire at the Pinova plant sent hazardous plumes of smoke across nearby
                            neighborhoods, forcing residents to seek shelter.</p>

                        <h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-restoring-black-excellence-in-sweet-auburn">Restoring Black
                            Excellence In Sweet Auburn</h2>



                        <p>In the heart of Atlanta lies a stark reminder of how poorly planned infrastructure can harm
                            community prosperity. Sweet Auburn Avenue, once dubbed &#8220;the richest Negro street in
                            America,&#8221; stood as a testament to Black entrepreneurship and resilience during the Jim
                            Crow era. Here, Black-owned insurance companies, banks, and entertainment venues created a
                            thriving ecosystem of commerce and culture.</p>



                        <p>Then came the highway.</p>

                        <p>In the mid-1960s, the Interstate 75/85 &#8220;downtown connector&#8221; tore through Sweet
                            Auburn. The construction displaced an estimated 30,000 residents and dealt a deadly blow to
                            the district&#8217;s vitality. With no direct highway access to Auburn Avenue, traffic was
                            deliberately diverted around the community. Bustling storefronts were replaced by empty lots
                            and boarded-up buildings. This physical divide created by the highway resulted in decades of
                            disinvestment and disregard, marking the end of Sweet Auburn&#8217;s golden age.</p>



                        <p>&#8220;Sweet Auburn was an extremely vibrant and important community to the Black
                            nation,&#8221; Smith explains. &#8220;But in the eyes of white leadership, they still
                            considered it blighted, which they used as an excuse to build a highway through Auburn
                            Avenue.&#8221;</p>



                        <p>Today, a coalition of organizations is working to heal this rift. PSE has joined forces with
                            Perkins &amp; Will and Nelson\Nygaard in the Reconnecting Sweet Auburn Project, an
                            initiative funded by Invest Atlanta, Sweet Auburn Works, and the Atlanta Downtown
                            Improvement District (ADID).</p>



                        <p>The team has set specific goals: documenting the long-term negative effects of the highway
                            construction using hard data about physical infrastructure, environmental conditions, and
                            demographic changes. They&#8217;re also studying successful examples nationwide of how other
                            divided communities have rebuilt and reconnected. In late 2024, the project took a major
                            step forward, applying for an $800,000 Reconnecting Communities Pilot Grant to advance their
                            design recommendations.</p>



                        <p>The project also aims to deliver immediate, community-led design interventions to improve
                            quality of life while developing longer-term strategies to reimagine Sweet Auburn&#8217;s
                            relationship with the highway that divided it. This includes the chance to reconfigure
                            existing infrastructure, like streets, ramps, and bridges, to make the neighborhood more
                            livable and attract new investment.</p>



                        <p>The challenge now is ensuring Sweet Auburn&#8217;s revival benefits its existing community
                            rather than pushing them out. As neighborhoods improve, rising property values often
                            displace the very residents who weathered decades of disinvestment. But Smith is confident
                            in his community-first approach. &#8220;We believe that a resilience agenda cannot be fully
                            realized without a healing and repair agenda,” he says. “It&#8217;s not just about
                            beautifying places—it&#8217;s about healing people and positioning people to be
                            successful.&#8221;</p>



                        <h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-data-driven-path-forward">A Data-Driven Path Forward</h2>



                        <p>PSE&#8217;s approach has already shown concrete results. In Atlanta, where 90% of major job
                            centers are located north of Interstate 20 while many underserved communities live south of
                            it, the organization achieved its first major policy win by expanding public transportation.
                            Working with faith communities and young people, PSE launched &#8220;The Power of the
                            Penny&#8221; campaign, convincing residents to support a one-cent sales tax for transit
                            expansion. The initiative secured over 70% voter approval, creating a $13 million fund to
                            extend MARTA (Metropolitan Atlanta Rapid Transit Authority). This wasn&#8217;t just about
                            transportation &#8211; with more cars sitting in traffic due to a lack of accessible transit
                            options, Atlanta&#8217;s carbon footprint was expanding and residents, especially children
                            and seniors, were suffering from increased air pollution. PSE&#8217;s success demonstrated
                            how addressing transportation equity could simultaneously tackle environmental justice and
                            public health.</p>



                        <p>PSE&#8217;s success offers a clear blueprint for addressing environmental challenges across
                            the South. Their model, supported by Skoll Foundation funding, demonstrates how combining
                            health data, community leadership, and environmental advocacy creates measurable change.
                            This approach has already helped over a hundred organizations secure $60 million through
                            former President Biden&#8217;s Justice 40 Executive Order, showing how local successes can
                            scale to regional impact.</p>



                        <p>&#8220;Equity is love in action,&#8221; explains Smith. &#8220;We can&#8217;t facilitate an
                            environment where all people have a chance to reach their full potential if we&#8217;re not
                            courageous enough to love everybody.&#8221; This philosophy drives PSE&#8217;s commitment to
                            ensuring communities lead their transformation, from fighting utility rate increases that
                            burden low-income residents to developing the South&#8217;s first equity mapping tool.</p>



                        <p>For PSE, the future is about continuing to expand its impact across the South while staying
                            true to its core principle that those closest to problems are closest to solutions. Their
                            experience shows that successful environmental justice work requires more than just data and
                            policy change – it demands creating spaces where local community members can unite to build
                            a more equitable future.</p>

document.addEventListener('alpine:init', () => {
    Alpine.data('browseCollectionList', () => (
        {
            loading: false, // used to prevent multiple clicks on load more button
            page: 1, // used to track the page number 
            showLoadMoreButton: true, // used to show/hide the load more button
            perPage: 3, // must align with the per_page parameter in initial render
            /**
             * Hit colleciton API endpoint with new page
             */
            async getNewbatch() {
                const url = `/wp-json/freethink/v1/collection/78990?page=${this.page}&per_page=${this.perPage}`;
                try {
                    const response = await fetch(url);
                    return await response.json()
                } catch (error) {
                    console.info('Collection endpoint API download error: ', error);
                }
                return null;
            },
            /**
             * Load more items into the collection list
             */
            async loadMore() {
                if (!getCollectionListItemTemplate) {
                    return;
                }
                if (this.loading) {
                    return;
                }
                this.loading = true;
                const newBatch = await this.getNewbatch();
                if (newBatch && newBatch.items && newBatch.items.length > 0) {
                    // add new items to existing list
                    let markup = '';
                    newBatch.items.forEach(item => {
                        const nextItemMarkup = getCollectionListItemTemplate(item);
                        if (nextItemMarkup) {   
                            markup += nextItemMarkup;
                        }
                    });
                    const loadMore = this.$refs.browseCollection.querySelector("#loadMoreBtn");
                    if (markup && loadMore) {
                        loadMore.insertAdjacentHTML(
                            'beforebegin',
                            markup
                        );
                    }
                }

                // hide load more button if no more items
                // count the number of hb-collection-list__item elements in this.$refs.browseCollection
                // if less than newBatch.found_posts than show button
                const collectionItems = this.$refs.browseCollection.querySelectorAll('.hb-collection-list__item');
                this.showLoadMoreButton = (collectionItems && collectionItems.length && newBatch && newBatch.found_posts && collectionItems.length < newBatch.found_posts);
            
                this.page++;
                this.loading = false;
            },
        }
    ));
})
